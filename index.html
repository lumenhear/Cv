Our Clinical Services

Comprehensive diagnostic and therapeutic services for all ages

Pure Tone Audiometry (PTA)

The gold standard for hearing assessment. We measure your hearing sensitivity across different frequencies to determine the type and degree of hearing loss. This painless test helps us create your personalized hearing profile.

Diagnostic

Impedance Audiometry

Evaluates middle ear function to identify conditions like fluid buildup, eardrum perforation, or ossicular chain problems. Essential for comprehensive hearing assessment and treatment planning.

Diagnostic

Otoacoustic Emissions (OAE)

Quick, non-invasive test measuring inner ear (cochlear) function. Especially valuable for newborn hearing screening and early detection of hearing loss in young children.

Screening

BERA (Brainstem Evoked Response Audiometry)

Advanced neurological test assessing the auditory pathway from ear to brain. Critical for diagnosing auditory neuropathy, determining hearing thresholds in infants, and evaluating neurological function.

Advanced

BC-ABR (Bone Conduction ABR)

Specialized ABR test using bone conduction to distinguish between conductive and sensorineural hearing loss. Invaluable for precise diagnosis and treatment planning.

Advanced

CHOCHG (Cochlear Hydrops Analysis)

Sophisticated testing for inner ear disorders like Meniere's disease. Helps identify abnormal fluid pressure in the cochlea, guiding treatment for vertigo and fluctuating hearing loss.

Specialized

Hearing Aid Fitting

Comprehensive hearing aid selection, fitting, and programming. We work with premium brands to find the perfect device for your hearing loss, lifestyle, and budget. Includes real-ear measurements and fine-tuning.

Therapeutic

Hearing Aid Repair & Service

Expert maintenance, cleaning, and repair services for all hearing aid brands. From simple battery replacements to complex electronic repairs, we keep your devices functioning optimally.

Maintenance

Speech Therapy

Hearing Aid Solutions

Advanced technology for every hearing need and lifestyle

Types of Hearing Aids

Behind-The-Ear (BTE)

Powerful and versatile, suitable for all degrees of hearing loss. Comfortable, easy to handle, and packed with advanced features.

Receiver-In-Canal (RIC)

Discreet yet powerful design with natural sound quality. Perfect balance of cosmetics and performance.

In-The-Ear (ITE)

Custom-made to fit your ear perfectly. Easier to handle than smaller styles while remaining discreet.

In-The-Canal (ITC)

Smaller and less visible, custom-fitted to your ear canal. Great for mild to moderate hearing loss.

Completely-In-Canal (CIC)

Nearly invisible option fitting deep in the ear canal. Maximum discretion for mild to moderate loss.

Invisible-In-Canal (IIC)

The most discreet option, sitting deep in the ear canal. Truly invisible hearing solution.

Disorders We Treat

Expert diagnosis and treatment for a wide range of hearing and communication disorders

Hearing Disorders

Conductive Hearing Loss

What it is: Hearing loss caused by problems in the outer or middle ear that prevent sound from reaching the inner ear.

Signs: Muffled hearing, difficulty hearing soft sounds, pain or pressure in ears, drainage from ears.

Causes: Ear infections, fluid buildup, eardrum perforation, earwax blockage, ossicular chain damage.

Treatment: Often medically treatable. We provide comprehensive assessment, medical referrals when needed, and hearing aids if necessary.

Sensorineural Hearing Loss

What it is: Permanent hearing loss due to damage to the inner ear (cochlea) or auditory nerve.

Signs: Difficulty understanding speech especially in noise, sounds seem muffled, asking people to repeat frequently.

Causes: Aging, noise exposure, genetics, viral infections, ototoxic medications.

Treatment: Hearing aids, assistive devices, auditory training, and counseling for communication strategies.

Mixed Hearing Loss

What it is: Combination of conductive and sensorineural hearing loss affecting both outer/middle and inner ear.

Assessment: Comprehensive audiometry including bone conduction testing to identify both components.

Treatment: May require medical management for conductive component plus hearing aids for sensorineural component.

Noise-Induced Hearing Loss

What it is: Permanent hearing damage from exposure to loud sounds, either sudden or prolonged.

Prevention: Hearing protection counseling, custom ear plugs for high-noise environments.

Management: Hearing aids, tinnitus management if present, hearing conservation programs.

Presbycusis (Age-Related Hearing Loss)

What it is: Gradual hearing loss that occurs as we age, typically affecting high frequencies first.

Signs: Difficulty with high-pitched sounds, trouble understanding speech in background noise, need for increased volume.

Management: Hearing aids, assistive listening devices, communication strategies, regular monitoring.

Auditory Neuropathy Spectrum Disorder (ANSD)

What it is: Disorder where sound enters the inner ear normally but transmission to the brain is impaired.

Diagnosis: Requires specialized testing including OAE and BERA to identify the characteristic pattern.

Management: Individualized approach including hearing aids, cochlear implant evaluation, auditory training, and visual communication strategies.

Tinnitus

What it is: Perception of ringing, buzzing, or other sounds in ears without external source.

Assessment: Comprehensive hearing evaluation, tinnitus matching and measurement.

Management: Sound therapy, tinnitus retraining therapy, hearing aids with masking features, counseling, relaxation techniques.

Hyperacusis

What it is: Increased sensitivity to everyday sounds that seem uncomfortably or painfully loud.

Management: Sound desensitization therapy, counseling, hearing protection guidance, gradual sound exposure therapy.

Speech Disorders

Articulation Disorder

What it is: Difficulty producing specific speech sounds correctly (substitutions, omissions, distortions).

Signs: Child says "wabbit" for "rabbit," omits sounds like "ca" for "cat," or distorts sounds.

Therapy: Targeted articulation exercises, auditory discrimination training, home practice programs. Most children show significant improvement with consistent therapy.

Phonological Disorder

What it is: Patterns of sound errors affecting multiple sounds (e.g., simplifying all words ending in consonants).

Assessment: Comprehensive phonological analysis to identify error patterns.

Therapy: Pattern-based approach targeting sound rules rather than individual sounds. Highly effective with structured intervention.

Childhood Apraxia of Speech

What it is: Motor planning disorder where brain has difficulty coordinating movements for speech.

Signs: Inconsistent errors, difficulty sequencing sounds, groping movements, prosody problems.

Therapy: Intensive motor-based approach focusing on movement sequences, PROMPT therapy, high practice frequency. Requires specialized expertise.

Dysarthria

What it is: Speech disorder from weakness or paralysis of speech muscles due to neurological damage.

Causes: Stroke, traumatic brain injury, cerebral palsy, Parkinson's disease, ALS.

Therapy: Strengthening exercises, breath support training, rate control, articulation strategies, augmentative communication if needed.

Stuttering (Fluency Disorder)

What it is: Disruption in the flow of speech with repetitions, prolongations, or blocks.

Signs: Sound/word repetitions, prolonged sounds, silent blocks, physical tension, avoidance behaviors.

Therapy: Fluency shaping techniques, stuttering modification, cognitive-behavioral approaches, parent training for children. Focus on reducing tension and building confidence.

Cluttering

What it is: Rapid, irregular speech rate affecting clarity and organization.

Therapy: Rate control strategies, self-monitoring training, organization and planning skills.

Voice Disorders

Types: Vocal nodules, polyps, laryngitis, vocal fatigue, muscle tension dysphonia.

Signs: Hoarseness, breathiness, vocal fatigue, pitch problems, loss of voice.

Assessment: Voice analysis, medical referral for laryngoscopy when indicated.

Therapy: Vocal hygiene education, resonant voice therapy, breath support training, relaxation techniques. Coordination with ENT specialist as needed.

Language Disorders

Receptive Language Disorder

What it is: Difficulty understanding and processing language.

Signs: Doesn't follow directions, answers questions inappropriately, limited vocabulary comprehension, trouble understanding complex sentences.

Therapy: Vocabulary building, comprehension strategies, following directions activities, parent coaching for language-rich environment.

Expressive Language Disorder

What it is: Difficulty using words and sentences to communicate thoughts.

Signs: Limited vocabulary, short sentences, grammatical errors, word-finding difficulties, trouble organizing thoughts.

Therapy: Vocabulary expansion, sentence building, grammar instruction, narrative skills development, conversational practice.

Mixed Receptive-Expressive Disorder

What it is: Difficulties with both understanding and using language.

Therapy: Comprehensive language intervention addressing both comprehension and expression, with emphasis on functional communication.

Late Language Emergence

What it is: Delayed language development in toddlers who are otherwise developing normally ("late talkers").

Assessment: Developmental screening, hearing evaluation, comprehensive language testing.

Approach: Early intervention crucial. Parent-implemented strategies, play-based therapy, naturalistic teaching methods.

Specific Language Impairment (SLI)

What it is: Language difficulties without other developmental delays or known causes.

Therapy: Intensive language intervention, grammar training, vocabulary development, narrative skills. Long-term support often needed.

Developmental Language Disorder

What it is: Persistent language difficulties affecting academic and social success.

Management: Ongoing therapy, school collaboration, accommodations, strategy instruction for academic success.

Neurological Communication Disorders

Aphasia

What it is: Language impairment from brain damage, typically stroke.

Types:

  • Broca's: Difficulty speaking but comprehension relatively intact
  • Wernicke's: Fluent but meaningless speech with poor comprehension
  • Global: Severe impairment in all language areas
  • Anomic: Word-finding difficulties

Therapy: Individualized approach focusing on functional communication, compensatory strategies, family training, and intensive practice.

Cognitive Communication Disorders

What it is: Communication problems from cognitive deficits (attention, memory, executive function).

Causes: Traumatic brain injury, dementia, right hemisphere stroke.

Therapy: Cognitive-communication strategies, memory aids, attention training, social pragmatics.

Parkinson's-Related Speech Issues

Signs: Soft voice, monotone speech, rapid speech, imprecise articulation.

Therapy: LSVT LOUD therapy (evidence-based), breath support, articulation exercises, rate control.

Post-Stroke Speech Impairments

Types: May include aphasia, dysarthria, apraxia, or combination.

Approach: Comprehensive assessment, intensive early intervention, family training, long-term support for maximum recovery.

Pediatric Disorders

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) Communication

Challenges: Social communication deficits, pragmatic language difficulties, repetitive language, echolalia.

Assessment: Comprehensive language and pragmatic evaluation, social communication observation.

Therapy: Social communication groups, pragmatic language training, augmentative communication if needed, naturalistic teaching, parent coaching. Focus on functional communication and social interaction.

Progress: Varies widely; consistent therapy with family involvement yields best outcomes.

ADHD-Related Communication Delays

Challenges: Difficulty following conversations, interrupting, disorganized communication, poor listening skills.

Therapy: Attention training, organizational strategies, conversational skills, self-monitoring techniques.

Pediatric Hearing Loss

Impact: Can significantly affect speech and language development if untreated.

Management: Early identification through screening, hearing aid fitting, auditory-verbal therapy, speech therapy, family support and education.

Importance: Early intervention (before age 1) critical for normal language development.

Speech Delay

What it is: Speech development slower than peers but following typical pattern.

Assessment: Hearing screening, oral-motor evaluation, developmental assessment.

Therapy: Play-based articulation therapy, oral-motor exercises, parent training in stimulation techniques.

Language Delay

What it is: Overall language skills (vocabulary, grammar, comprehension) behind age expectations.

Evaluation: Comprehensive language testing, hearing assessment, developmental screening.

Therapy: Early intervention services, language stimulation, parent coaching, structured language activities.

Prognosis: With early intervention, many children catch up to peers. Ongoing monitoring important.
